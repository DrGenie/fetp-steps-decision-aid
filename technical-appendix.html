1. Introduction and purpose of the STEPS tool

The STEPS FETP India Decision Aid is an interactive decision support tool that combines preference evidence, cost data and outbreak impact assumptions to help policy makers design and assess Field Epidemiology Training Programme scale up scenarios in India.

The tool has three linked components. First, it uses a discrete choice experiment and mixed logit model to quantify how key stakeholder groups value different FETP programme designs, including training tier, mentoring intensity, delivery mode and qualification. Second, it applies harmonised cost templates derived from World Health Organization, National Institute of Epidemiology and National Centre for Disease Control data to translate a monthly cost per trainee into a full cost envelope for each tier, including indirect and opportunity costs. Third, it maps training capacity into outbreak responses and monetary benefits, using a literature based calibration for the economic value of outbreaks, an explicit planning horizon and discount rate, and a transparent response time multiplier.

The STEPS tool is scenario based. Users specify a combination of programme design attributes and cost, along with the number of cohorts, cohort size, planning horizon and whether opportunity costs are included. The tool then produces endorsement probabilities from the mixed logit model, implied willingness to pay, epidemiological benefits, benefit cost ratios and net benefits for the configured scenario. All outputs are stylised decision support indicators rather than forecasts of realised costs or savings.

This technical appendix documents how each part of the tool works. It explains the preference model, programme attributes, cost construction, benefit calculations, sensitivity analysis and the Copilot interpretation tab. The aim is that a technically trained policy analyst can understand, reproduce and defend the calculations without inspecting the code.

2. Overview of the preference study and mixed logit model

2.1 Discrete choice experiment and design

Preferences for FETP scale up options were measured using a discrete choice experiment in which stakeholders evaluated hypothetical FETP programmes that differed along key design attributes. Each choice task asked respondents to choose between two alternative FETP programme designs and an opt out option in which no additional FETP training would be funded.

The experimental design varied the following attributes.

Tier of training Frontline, intermediate, advanced.

Mentoring intensity Low, medium, high.

Delivery mode Blended, fully in person, fully online.

Qualification Government and partner certificate, university qualification, government career pathway recognition.

Outbreak response time Time taken to detect and respond to priority outbreaks: 30 days, 15 days or 7 days.

Monthly cost per trainee Different levels in Indian Rupees.

Attribute levels were selected through expert consultation and piloting to span realistic, policy relevant FETP designs while avoiding implausible combinations. Cost levels were chosen to support robust estimation of willingness to pay for improvements in mentoring, delivery, qualification and response time.

2.2 Mixed logit model specification

Choice data from the discrete choice experiment were analysed using a mixed logit model that allows preferences to vary across respondents. For use in the STEPS tool, the mean preference estimates from the mixed logit model are used as fixed coefficients. These coefficients define the utility of each programme option and the opt out option as linear functions of the attributes.

For a programme option, the indirect utility combines an alternative specific constant, indicator variables for attribute levels and a cost term expressed per thousand rupees per trainee per month. For the opt out option, utility is represented by a single constant that reflects the baseline attractiveness of not implementing additional FETP training relative to the status quo.

2.4 Endorsement and opt out probabilities

For a given configuration in STEPS, the programme utility and opt out utility are combined to obtain endorsement and opt out probabilities. The programme utility adds the programme constant, the relevant attribute effects for tier, qualification, mentorship, delivery and response time, and the cost term based on the selected monthly cost per trainee. The opt out utility is given by the opt out constant. The tool rescales utilities to avoid numerical overflow, then converts them to probabilities using the standard two option logit formula. These probabilities are reported as endorsement and opt out rates.

3. Programme design attributes and fixed response time

Programme design attributes in the tool mirror those in the preference study. The delivery mode, mentoring intensity, qualification and tier defining each configuration are therefore directly linked to the mixed logit coefficients. Response time is constrained in the current version of the tool.

Users select the tier, career incentive, mentoring intensity and delivery mode on the configuration tab. In the current implementation, the response time attribute is fixed to seven days and the corresponding programme coefficient is applied. The response time dropdown is visible but locked so that the choice remains consistent with the epidemiological module, which assumes rapid response.

Trainees per cohort, the number of cohorts and the planning horizon are user inputs. These values do not enter the utility function directly, but they scale costs, willingness to pay and epidemiological outputs. Opportunity cost is controlled by a switch that determines whether the opportunity cost component in the cost templates is included in the economic cost concept.

4. Cost construction methodology

Cost calculations in STEPS start from a user selected monthly cost per trainee and scale this to cohort level programme cost and total economic cost using harmonised cost templates.

Cost templates are defined separately for frontline, intermediate and advanced tiers. Each template is based on combined empirical cost structures across institutions, covering programme staff, travel, training materials, trainee support, office costs and other direct expenses. Each cost component has a direct cost share and the template includes an opportunity cost rate that expresses the value of trainee time spent in training as a percentage of the direct programme cost.

The user selects a tier and a per trainee per month cost using the cost slider. The tool multiplies this amount by the duration of the tier and the number of trainees per cohort to obtain a direct programme cost per cohort. This direct cost is then split across cost components according to the template shares and combined with the opportunity cost rate when the opportunity cost switch is on.

6. Programme cost per trainee and per cohort

Programme cost calculations in STEPS follow a simple scaling logic that links the user selected cost per trainee per month to per cohort costs and national totals.

6.1 Tier durations

The tool assumes fixed durations for each tier expressed in months.

6.2 Programme cost per cohort

For a configuration with cost per trainee per month, programme duration and number of trainees per cohort, the tool calculates programme cost per cohort as the product of these three elements. This gives the direct financial cost of running one cohort at the specified cost level and scale.

6.3 Total economic cost per cohort

Total economic cost per cohort adds opportunity cost of trainee time when the opportunity cost switch is on. Opportunity cost is calculated by applying the tier specific opportunity cost rate to the direct programme cost per cohort. When the switch is off, the opportunity cost rate is set to zero and total economic cost equals the direct programme cost.

7. Opportunity cost inclusion and exclusion

Opportunity cost treatment in STEPS is controlled by a switch that allows users to include or exclude opportunity cost of trainee time in the economic cost concept.

When opportunity cost is included, the tool interprets the selected cost per trainee per month as a financial cost and then applies the tier specific opportunity cost rate to derive additional economic cost associated with trainee time spent in training rather than their usual duties. This additional cost is added to the programme cost per cohort to obtain total economic cost per cohort.

When opportunity cost is excluded, the opportunity cost rate is treated as zero and total economic cost per cohort equals the direct programme cost per cohort. All benefit cost ratios and net benefits use the economic cost concept, so the opportunity cost switch influences regional and national summaries and the sensitivity analysis.

8. Willingness to pay calculations

Willingness to pay indicators in STEPS are derived from the mixed logit coefficients and summarise how much value stakeholders place on a configuration, expressed in rupees per trainee per month and aggregated across cohorts.

8.1 Non cost utility and cost coefficient

For a given configuration, the tool first calculates the non cost part of the programme utility by adding the programme constant and all relevant attribute coefficients for tier, qualification, mentoring intensity, delivery mode and response time. It then divides this non cost utility by the absolute value of the cost coefficient and scales by one thousand to obtain an indicative willingness to pay per trainee per month in rupees. This amount is interpreted as the model implied rupee value respondents attach to the chosen combination of attribute levels per trainee per month.

8.2 WTP per cohort and across cohorts

Willingness to pay per trainee per month is multiplied by the duration of the tier and by the number of trainees per cohort to obtain a willingness to pay per cohort. This cohort level amount is then multiplied by the number of cohorts to obtain total willingness to pay across all cohorts in the configuration. The tool reports these values in the results and national simulation tabs and uses them in the sensitivity analysis.

In addition, the tool defines a simple outbreak related component of willingness to pay by allocating a fixed proportion of total willingness to pay across cohorts to outbreak response and detection. This component is used only in the sensitivity analysis to give a sense of how much of the preference based value might be linked to outbreak functions. It does not change any of the core epidemiological benefit calculations.

9. Epidemiological benefit construction

The epidemiological module in STEPS translates cohorts and completion rates into numbers of graduates and outbreak responses and then applies a value per outbreak over a selected planning horizon.

9.1 General settings

General settings specify the planning horizon in years, the discount rate for epidemiological benefits and the exchange rate for converting rupees to United States dollars when needed. These settings apply to all tiers. The planning horizon and discount rate together define a present value factor that is used to convert a future stream of outbreak benefits into a single present value.

9.2 Tier specific epidemiological parameters

For each tier, the tool sets default values for completion rate, expected number of outbreaks handled per graduate per year and value per graduate and per outbreak. In the current implementation, value per graduate is set to zero and all epidemiological monetary benefits are driven by outbreak responses and the assumed value per outbreak. Completion rates and outbreaks per graduate per year are common across tiers by default but can be updated in the settings.

9.3 Graduates and outbreak responses

For a given configuration, the tool calculates the number of graduates per cohort by multiplying the number of trainees per cohort by the completion rate and by the endorsement rate expressed as a proportion. This gives the effective number of graduates produced by one cohort under the chosen configuration. The number of graduates across all cohorts is obtained by multiplying this amount by the number of cohorts.

Outbreak responses per year per cohort are calculated by multiplying graduates per cohort by the assumed number of outbreaks handled per graduate per year and by a response time multiplier. The response time multiplier increases the credited number of outbreak responses when faster response times are selected. In the current implementation, response time is fixed at seven days and the corresponding multiplier is applied. Outbreak responses per year at national level are obtained by multiplying the cohort level amount by the number of cohorts.

9.4 Monetary value of outbreak responses

The tool applies a value per outbreak to outbreak responses per year per cohort and multiplies this by the present value factor implied by the planning horizon and discount rate. The resulting amount is the outbreak related epidemiological benefit per cohort. This benefit is interpreted as an indicative monetary value of reduced outbreak impact and is not a forecast of actual savings. The value per outbreak can be set directly or via presets corresponding to different orders of magnitude, such as four, five, ten, twenty, thirty and forty million rupees.

10. Value per outbreak calibration and evidence base

The value per outbreak in STEPS is an assumed monetary value used for scenario analysis rather than a direct empirical estimate for India. It is calibrated using evidence on economic losses associated with outbreaks and pandemics, combined with judgement about the scale of events that FETP graduates can influence.

Users can adjust the value per outbreak in rupees or via drop down presets that correspond to rounded values in millions of rupees. Internally, the tool converts all entries into rupees and applies the same value per outbreak across tiers unless advanced settings are used to specify tier specific values. The sensitivity analysis tab allows users to explore how results change as the value per outbreak is varied across a plausible range.

11. Benefit cost ratios and net benefit

STEPS reports benefit cost ratios and net benefits at cohort and national level based on the outbreak related epidemiological benefits and total economic costs.

11.1 Per cohort indicators

For each configuration, the tool compares the outbreak related epidemiological benefit per cohort with the total economic cost per cohort. The benefit cost ratio per cohort is the outbreak benefit divided by the total economic cost, while the net benefit per cohort is the difference between these two amounts. Ratios above one and positive net benefits indicate that outbreak related benefits exceed economic costs under the current assumptions. Ratios below one and negative net benefits indicate that costs exceed outbreak benefits when value per outbreak, planning horizon and discount rate are held fixed.

11.2 National indicators

National indicators aggregate outbreak benefits and costs across cohorts. Total economic cost across all cohorts equals total economic cost per cohort multiplied by the number of cohorts. Total outbreak benefit equals outbreak benefit per cohort multiplied by the number of cohorts. The national benefit cost ratio is the ratio of these two amounts and the national net benefit is their difference. National graduates and outbreak responses per year are also reported.

12. National simulation scaling

The national simulation tab in STEPS summarises how a configuration scales when multiple cohorts are run and expresses totals in rupees and, when needed, in millions of rupees.

The tab reports total economic cost, total outbreak related benefit, national benefit cost ratio, national net benefit, total graduates, outbreak responses per year and total willingness to pay, all aggregated across cohorts. The narrative text on the tab explains how these indicators relate to the building blocks in the configuration and settings and highlights that all monetary values depend on the chosen value per outbreak and other epidemiological assumptions.

13. Sensitivity analysis framework

The sensitivity analysis in STEPS focuses on how headline results change when outbreak values, endorsement assumptions and definitions of benefits based on willingness to pay are varied.

13.1 Benefit definitions

The sensitivity tab allows users to choose between different benefit definitions. One definition uses willingness to pay only, where total willingness to pay across cohorts is compared with total economic cost. Another definition adjusts total willingness to pay by the endorsement rate so that the benefit reflects only the fraction of stakeholders who are expected to endorse the configuration. The epidemiological benefit component from outbreak responses can be included or excluded using a switch, so that benefits can reflect willingness to pay alone, outbreak benefits alone or a combination.

13.2 Endorsement overrides

Users can override the endorsement rate implied by the mixed logit model with a different value when producing sensitivity summaries. The tool then scales willingness to pay by this override and recomputes benefit cost ratios and net present values for each scenario in the summary tables. This feature helps users explore scenarios where political or institutional support differs from the average endorsement predicted by the preference study.

13.3 Outbreak value sensitivity

The sensitivity tab includes controls for adjusting the value per outbreak via presets and free text entries. These controls update the common value per outbreak across tiers and recompute outbreak related benefits and all dependent indicators. The sensitivity tables then summarise costs, willingness to pay, outbreak related benefits, effective willingness to pay and benefit cost ratios in both rupees and millions of rupees for the current configuration and any saved scenarios.

14. Worked example: intermediate, high mentoring, blended delivery

This section gives a worked example using an intermediate tier configuration with high mentoring, blended delivery and a specified monthly cost per trainee. All numerical values are for illustration; the actual numbers in the tool will depend on the exact inputs chosen in the configuration and settings tabs.

14.1 Scenario description

Consider an intermediate tier FETP with the following features: government and partner certificate as the qualification, high mentoring intensity, blended delivery and detection and response within seven days. Suppose there are 30 trainees per cohort, two cohorts, a monthly cost per trainee of 50 000 rupees and a planning horizon of five years. Assume the default completion rate of 90 percent, an average of 0.5 outbreaks handled per graduate per year and a value per outbreak of four million rupees. Opportunity cost is included.

14.2 Step 1: Preference model and endorsement

The non cost part of the programme utility combines the programme constant, the intermediate tier coefficient, the high mentoring coefficient, the blended delivery reference level and the seven day response coefficient. The tool adds these effects and then subtracts the product of the cost coefficient and the cost per trainee per month expressed in thousands of rupees. This gives the programme utility. The opt out utility is given by the opt out constant. The tool converts these utilities into endorsement and opt out probabilities using the two option logit share, rescales them to percentages and reports them as endorsement and opt out rates.

14.3 Step 2: Programme cost and opportunity cost

The programme cost per cohort is the monthly cost per trainee multiplied by the programme duration for the intermediate tier and by the number of trainees per cohort. The opportunity cost per cohort is then obtained by multiplying this programme cost by the intermediate tier opportunity cost rate. These two components sum to the total economic cost per cohort, which is shown in the results, costing and national tabs.

14.4 Step 3: Willingness to pay

The tool calculates willingness to pay per trainee per month by dividing the non cost utility by the absolute value of the cost coefficient and scaling by one thousand. This per trainee per month amount is then multiplied by the programme duration and the number of trainees per cohort to obtain willingness to pay per cohort. Multiplying by the number of cohorts gives total willingness to pay across all cohorts. The tool reports these values and uses them in the sensitivity tables where they are compared with total economic costs.

14.5 Step 4: Graduates and outbreak responses

Graduates per cohort are obtained by multiplying the number of trainees per cohort by the completion rate and by the endorsement rate expressed as a proportion. Multiplying this amount by the number of cohorts gives total graduates. Outbreak responses per year per cohort are calculated as graduates per cohort times 0.5 outbreaks per graduate per year and the response time multiplier for seven days. Multiplying this amount by the number of cohorts gives outbreak responses per year at national level for the configuration.

14.6 Step 5: Outbreak related benefits, benefit cost ratios and net benefits

The tool multiplies outbreak responses per year per cohort by the value per outbreak to obtain an annual outbreak related benefit per cohort and then multiplies this by the present value factor implied by the five year planning horizon and the chosen discount rate. This gives the outbreak related epidemiological benefit per cohort. The benefit cost ratio per cohort is the ratio of this amount to the total economic cost per cohort and the net benefit per cohort is the difference between them. The same logic applies at national level after multiplying benefits and costs by the number of cohorts.

This worked example illustrates the flow of calculations without showing exact intermediate numerical values, which will change with different cost levels, cohort sizes, value per outbreak and discount rates. Users can reproduce these calculations by reading the configuration and settings from the tool and applying the steps described in this section in a spreadsheet or statistical package.

15. Copilot interpretation and JSON export

The Copilot tab in STEPS prepares a structured prompt and a JSON export of the current scenario so that users can generate a narrative policy brief using Microsoft Copilot.

15.1 Scenario JSON

When the Copilot button is pressed, the tool constructs a JSON object that summarises the configuration, preference model outputs, cost results, epidemiological results and benefit cost indicators for the current scenario. The JSON includes metadata such as the generation time, country, planning horizon and discount rate, as well as configuration details like tier, career incentive, mentoring intensity, delivery mode, response time, cost per trainee per month, cohort size, number of cohorts and whether opportunity cost is included.

The JSON also reports endorsement and opt out rates, willingness to pay per trainee per month, willingness to pay per cohort, total willingness to pay across cohorts, programme costs, opportunity costs, economic costs, graduates, outbreak responses, outbreak related benefits and benefit cost ratios at cohort and national level. This JSON is inserted into the prompt between markers so that Copilot can treat it as the quantitative evidence base for the policy brief.

15.2 Interpretation prompt

The Copilot tab combines the scenario JSON with a text prompt that instructs Copilot to prepare a three to five page policy brief in narrative format. The suggested sections include background, scenario description, preference study evidence and endorsement, economic costs, epidemiological effects, benefit cost results, distributional and implementation considerations and recommendations. The prompt asks Copilot to use the JSON values consistently, to work in rupees and millions of rupees where helpful and to include at most one or two compact tables for clarity.

After generating the prompt, STEPS copies it to the clipboard when possible and opens Microsoft Copilot in a new browser tab. Users can then paste the prompt into Copilot, run it and edit the resulting draft in Copilot or in their preferred word processor, keeping the JSON values as a record of the assumptions used.

16. Summary of assumptions and limitations

The STEPS tool is designed as a practical decision aid for FETP scale up rather than a full economic evaluation model. Several simplifying assumptions are therefore built into the calculations.

First, the preference model uses mean mixed logit coefficients as fixed values and does not propagate uncertainty around these estimates into endorsement probabilities or willingness to pay indicators. Second, outbreak related benefits are based on a single value per outbreak and constant outbreaks per graduate per year, rather than a full transmission model with explicit uncertainty. Third, completion rates and outbreak response capacity are assumed to be constant across cohorts and tiers by default, although advanced settings allow users to adjust these parameters for sensitivity analysis.

Fourth, opportunity cost of trainee time is represented through a fixed rate applied to direct programme costs, which simplifies the underlying labour market and service delivery context. Fifth, all monetary values are expressed in nominal rupees and are not adjusted for future inflation or changes in health system costs over time. Finally, the sensitivity analysis framework is stylised and focuses on outbreak values, endorsement assumptions and willingness to pay definitions; it does not incorporate full probabilistic uncertainty analysis.

These limitations mean that STEPS should be interpreted as a structured way to compare policy relevant configurations and to explore how costs, outbreak related benefits and stated preferences interact. It is not a substitute for detailed programme budgeting or epidemiological modelling, but it can help ministries of health and finance, World Bank teams and other partners frame discussions about FETP scale up strategies and identify scenarios where more detailed analysis would be most valuable.
