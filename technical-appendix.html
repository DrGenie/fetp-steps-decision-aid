Technical appendix: how the STEPS FETP India tool does the calculations

This appendix explains how the STEPS tool converts program scenarios into: predicted endorsement, willingness to pay (WTP), training costs, outbreak response benefits, present values and benefit cost ratios. All numbers and coefficients below match the current script.js.

1. Preference model used in the STEPS tool

The tool is built on a mixed logit discrete choice experiment. For the appendix we use the mean coefficients from that model. Utility is measured on a latent scale; only differences in utility matter for choices.

1.1 Attributes and coefficients

The utility of joining a STEPS program has:

  • a constant for choosing any STEPS program (ascProgram = 0.168)
  • effects for each program attribute level
  • a cost term for the monthly training cost per trainee

Positive coefficients make programs more attractive, negative coefficients make them less attractive. The cost coefficient means that increasing the monthly cost by 1 000 rupees per trainee reduces utility by 0.005 units on the latent utility scale.

2. From a scenario to utility

For any scenario, the tool:

  1. Starts with the program constant 0.168.
  2. Adds the coefficient for the chosen level of each attribute.
  3. Subtracts the cost effect, which is the monthly cost (in thousand rupees) multiplied by 0.005.

We call the sum of the constant and all non cost terms the non cost utility U_non_cost. The full utility for the STEPS program is:

U_program = U_non_cost + (beta_cost × monthly_cost_in_thousand_rupees)

The opt out option has a fixed utility: U_opt_out = -0.601.

3. From utilities to predicted endorsement

The model uses a standard logit formula. Given the utility of the STEPS program U_program and the opt out utility U_opt_out, the probability that a representative stakeholder endorses the program is:

P(STEPS) = exp(U_program) / [exp(U_program) + exp(U_opt_out)]

The probability of not endorsing STEPS is 1 − P(STEPS). These probabilities drive the endorsement percentage shown in the configuration and results tabs.

4. From utilities to willingness to pay

The cost coefficient tells us how utility changes when the monthly cost per trainee changes. We interpret this as a marginal rate of substitution between the program attributes and cost.

The tool first computes U_non_cost for a scenario. The implied willingness to pay per trainee per month is:

WTP_per_trainee_per_month = U_non_cost ÷ |beta_cost| × 1 000

Since beta_cost = -0.005, a non cost utility of 1.0 corresponds to 1 ÷ 0.005 = 200 thousand rupees per trainee per month, or 200 000 rupees.

To obtain WTP per cohort, the tool multiplies this monthly WTP by:

  • program length in months
  • number of trainees per cohort

5. Direct and opportunity costs per cohort

The cost side follows simple accounting rules, scaled to a cohort of trainees. Program lengths by tier are:

Tier Program length (months)
Frontline 3
Intermediate 12
Advanced 24

For the worked examples below we assume a cohort size of 30 trainees. The direct training cost for one cohort is:

Direct cost = monthly cost per trainee × program months × cohort size

In addition, trainees devote time that could have been spent on their usual duties. We treat this as an opportunity cost equal to a fixed proportion of the direct cost:

Tier Opportunity cost as share of direct cost
Frontline 1.09
Intermediate 0.35
Advanced 0.30

The total economic cost of training one cohort is:

Total economic cost = Direct cost + Opportunity cost

6. Outbreak response benefits and present value

The benefit side links trained epidemiologists to improved outbreak response and monetary value. The tool uses the following assumptions:

  • Completion rate: 90 percent of enrolled trainees complete the program.
  • Each graduate contributes to 0.5 outbreaks managed per year on average.
  • Responding within 7 days multiplies this impact by a factor of 1.5.
  • The monetary value of managing one outbreak is 4 million rupees.

For a cohort with endorsement probability P(STEPS) and cohort size N, the expected number of effective graduates is:

Effective graduates = N × 0.9 × P(STEPS)

With a 7 day response capacity the expected outbreaks managed per year by this cohort are:

Outbreaks per year = Effective graduates × 0.5 × 1.5

The annual monetary benefit is:

Annual benefit = Outbreaks per year × 4 000 000 rupees

To account for time, the tool converts this constant annual stream of benefits into a five year present value using a discount rate of 3 percent per year. The present value factor is:

PV factor = (1 − (1 + r)−T) ÷ r with r = 0.03 and T = 5, which gives PV factor = 4.579708.

The present value of outbreak benefits for one cohort is:

PV benefit per cohort = Annual benefit × 4.579708

The benefit cost ratio (BCR) for one cohort is:

BCR = PV benefit per cohort ÷ Total economic cost per cohort

8. Worked example: Scenario S5

Scenario S5 is an intermediate tier program with medium mentoring, blended delivery, a government and partner certificate, outbreak response within 7 days and a monthly cost of 75 000 rupees per trainee. This section walks through the calculation step by step for a cohort of 30 trainees.

8.1 Utility and endorsement

First we sum the non cost terms:

  • Program constant: 0.168
  • Intermediate tier: 0.220
  • Medium mentoring: 0.453
  • Blended delivery: 0.000
  • Government and partner certificate: 0.000
  • Response within 7 days: 0.610

Adding these gives a non cost utility U_non_cost = 0.168 + 0.220 + 0.453 + 0 + 0 + 0.610 = 1.451. The monthly cost per trainee is 75 thousand rupees, so the cost term is beta_cost × 75 = -0.005 × 75 = -0.375. The full utility for the program is therefore U_program = 1.451 − 0.375 = 1.076.

The opt out utility is -0.601. The endorsement probability is:

P(STEPS) = exp(1.076) / [exp(1.076) + exp(-0.601)] = 0.8425

so about 84.3 percent of stakeholders are predicted to endorse this configuration.

8.2 WTP for S5

Using the non cost utility, the willingness to pay per trainee per month is:

WTP_per_trainee_per_month = 1.451 ÷ 0.005 × 1 000 = 290 200 rupees

For an intermediate program of 12 months and a cohort of 30 trainees, the implied WTP per cohort is:

WTP_per_cohort = 290 200 × 12 × 30 = 104 472 000 rupees which is about 104.472 million rupees.

8.3 Costs for S5

The direct training cost per cohort is:

Direct cost = 75 000 × 12 × 30 = 27 000 000 rupees

The opportunity cost for the intermediate tier is 35 percent of the direct cost:

Opportunity cost = 0.35 × 27 000 000 = 9 450 000 rupees

The total economic cost per cohort is therefore:

Total economic cost = 27 000 000 + 9 450 000 = 36 450 000 rupees which is 36.450 million rupees.

8.4 Outbreak benefits and present value for S5

With 30 trainees and a 90 percent completion rate, 27 trainees complete the program. Multiplying by the endorsement probability gives the expected number of effective graduates:

Effective graduates = 27 × 0.8425 = 22.7477

Each graduate manages 0.5 outbreaks per year, and the 7 day response capacity multiplies this by 1.5. The expected outbreaks managed per year by the cohort are:

Outbreaks per year = 22.7477 × 0.5 × 1.5 = 17.0608

With a value of 4 million rupees per outbreak, the annual monetary benefit is:

Annual benefit = 17.0608 × 4 000 000 = 68 243 056.70 rupees

Applying the five year present value factor of 4.579708 gives:

PV benefit per cohort = 68 243 056.70 × 4.579708 = 312 533 217.26 rupees

This is about 312.533 million rupees.

The benefit cost ratio for S5 is:

BCR = 312 533 217.26 ÷ 36 450 000 = 8.57

The net present benefit is the difference between benefits and total cost:

Net benefit = 312 533 217.26 − 36 450 000 = 276 083 217.26 rupees

so the program delivers a large surplus of benefits over costs in this scenario.

9. How these calculations feed into the STEPS tool

The main STEPS tabs read directly from these calculations:

  • The Configuration tab shows the endorsement percentage and WTP based on the utilities in section 2 and 3.
  • The Costs and Budget outputs use the direct and opportunity cost formulas in section 5 and scale them by the number of cohorts.
  • The Impact and Economic benefits sections use the outbreak response and present value logic in section 6.
  • The Sensitivity tab varies key parameters (cost, program tier, mentoring, number of cohorts and discount rate) and recomputes the same steps for each setting.
  • The Copilot tab packages the scenario specific numbers (endorsement, WTP, costs and present value benefits) into a JSON prompt that can be pasted into a generative tool to create narrative policy briefs.

10. Key assumptions and limitations

Finally, it is important to remember the main assumptions behind these numbers:

  • The preference parameters are estimated from a discrete choice experiment with a specific sample of stakeholders. Different samples could give slightly different coefficients.
  • The outbreak impact assumptions (0.5 outbreaks per graduate per year, 1.5 multiplier for a 7 day response and 4 million rupees per outbreak) are based on expert judgement and literature, and can be adjusted in future versions of the tool.
  • Opportunity cost shares by tier are stylised and aim to reflect typical time commitments of trainees. They are not accounting data from a specific program budget.
  • The present value factor is calculated for a constant benefit flow over five years with a fixed discount rate of 3 percent. A different horizon or rate would change the benefit numbers but not the underlying logic.

Users can therefore treat the values in this appendix as transparent worked examples that can be updated if better evidence on costs or epidemiological impacts becomes available.
