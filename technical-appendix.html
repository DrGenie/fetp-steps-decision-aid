1. Introduction and purpose of the STEPS tool

The STEPS FETP Scale Up Decision Aid is an interactive tool that combines preference evidence, cost data and outbreak impact assumptions to support decisions on scaling up Field Epidemiology Training Programmes in India.

The tool links three building blocks. First, a discrete choice experiment with a mixed logit model summarises how stakeholders value different FETP designs. Second, cost templates derived from programme data map a chosen monthly cost per trainee into full economic costs by tier. Third, epidemiological settings translate graduates into expected outbreak responses and monetary benefits using a user selected value per outbreak and a simple discounting rule.

Users define a scenario by choosing the training tier, career incentive, mentoring intensity, delivery mode, cohort size, number of cohorts, monthly cost per trainee and planning horizon, and by switching opportunity cost on or off. The tool then calculates endorsement and opt out shares, willingness to pay per trainee and for cohorts, programme and economic costs, outbreak related benefits and benefit cost ratios. All outputs are indicative and are designed to inform structured discussion with ministries and partners rather than to provide forecasts.

2. Preference study and mixed logit model

2.1 Discrete choice experiment

The discrete choice experiment asked stakeholders to choose between hypothetical FETP options and an opt out alternative in which no new FETP training would be funded. Each option was defined by the following attributes:

Tier of training Frontline, Intermediate, Advanced.

Mentoring intensity Low, Medium, High.

Delivery mode Blended, Fully in person, Fully online.

Career incentive Government and partner certificate; University qualification; Government career pathway.

Outbreak response time Time to detect and respond to a priority outbreak: 30 days, 15 days, or 7 days.

Monthly cost per trainee Different levels in Indian Rupees per trainee per month.

Attribute levels were chosen through expert consultation and piloting to span realistic and policy relevant designs. Cost levels were set to allow estimation of willingness to pay for changes in mentoring, delivery, career incentives and response time.

2.2 Mixed logit model structure

The tool relies on a mixed logit model estimated from the choice data. For the purpose of the web application, the mean coefficients from the mixed logit model are treated as fixed values. These coefficients define the utility of each programme option and of the opt out option.

The programme option utility combines an alternative specific constant, dummy coded levels for tier, career incentive, mentoring intensity, delivery mode and response time, and a linear cost term in thousands of Rupees per trainee per month. The opt out option has a single constant and no cost term. The mixed logit model in the application uses the following coefficients.

4. Cost construction and templates

Costing in STEPS is anchored on a user selected monthly cost per trainee. The tool uses harmonised cost templates to allocate this direct cost across detailed components and to define an opportunity cost rate for each tier. Templates have been constructed from programme budget and expenditure data and group similar items into common components. For each tier, the combined template has an opportunity cost rate and a set of direct cost shares that sum to one.

4.2 Direct cost component shares

For each tier, the template defines the share of direct programme cost allocated to each component. The underlying code stores these shares so that, once the user has chosen a cost per trainee per month, the tool can decompose the programme cost per cohort into detailed categories and calculate implied cost per trainee per month for each category.

For example, the advanced tier template includes components such as programme staff salaries and benefits, office equipment and software, rent and utilities, trainee allowances and scholarships, trainee equipment and software, training materials, workshops and seminars, travel in country and international travel, and other direct programme expenses. The sum of the direct shares equals one and the main cost components are displayed in the costing tab for transparency.

5. Programme cost per trainee and per cohort

5.1 Direct programme cost per cohort

The user selects a cost per trainee per month in Rupees. For a given tier, let the duration be D months and the number of trainees per cohort be N. The tool calculates the direct programme cost per cohort as:

Direct programme cost per cohort
Direct programme cost per cohort = Cost per trainee per month × Duration in months × Trainees per cohort

For example, if the cost per trainee per month is 30 000 Rupees, the duration is twelve months and there are 30 trainees, the direct programme cost per cohort is 30 000 multiplied by 12 multiplied by 30.

5.2 Component level breakdown

Once the direct cost per cohort is calculated, the template shares are used to obtain component level costs. For a component with a direct share of s, the cost allocated to that component is s multiplied by the direct programme cost per cohort. The per trainee per month value for that component is obtained by dividing the component cost by the product of trainees per cohort and duration in months.

The costing tab in the tool shows these decompositions for transparency, including the share of each component in total direct cost and its implied monthly value per trainee.

6. Opportunity cost of trainee time

Many FETP trainees are employed staff whose time spent in training has a value in terms of their normal duties. The tool captures this through an opportunity cost rate for each tier.

Economic cost per cohort
Opportunity cost per cohort = Direct programme cost per cohort × Opportunity cost rate Total economic cost per cohort = Direct programme cost per cohort + Opportunity cost per cohort

When the opportunity cost toggle is on, the economic cost includes this additional term. When the toggle is off, the opportunity cost rate is treated as zero and the economic cost equals the direct programme cost per cohort.

The results tab reports the direct programme cost per cohort and the total economic cost per cohort. The costing tab also reports the absolute value and percentage contribution of opportunity cost in the total economic cost.

7. Endorsement and opt out probabilities

For a given configuration the tool first constructs the utility for the programme option and for the opt out option using the mixed logit coefficients. Cost per trainee per month is converted to thousands of Rupees per trainee per month by dividing by 1 000 before applying the cost coefficient.

Programme and opt out utility
Programme utility = Programme constant * Tier effect * Career incentive effect * Mentorship effect * Delivery mode effect * Response time effect * Cost coefficient × (Cost per trainee per month / 1 000) Opt out utility = Opt out constant

Each effect is the corresponding coefficient if that level is selected, or zero otherwise. In the current version the configuration enforces a response time of seven days, so the response time effect in the programme utility is the coefficient for seven days.

The endorsement probability is the logit share of the programme option relative to the opt out option. The tool applies a common numerical stability adjustment by subtracting the maximum of the two utilities before exponentiation. The endorsement and opt out rates are then:

Endorsement and opt out rates
Endorsement probability = exp(Programme utility) / [exp(Programme utility) + exp(Opt out utility)] Opt out probability = 1 − Endorsement probability

These probabilities are then expressed as percentages for reporting. For example, an endorsement probability of 0.72 is reported as 72 percent.

The uptake chart in the results tab displays the endorsement and opt out shares side by side for ease of communication.

8. Willingness to pay calculations

8.1 Non cost utility and cost coefficient

The tool derives willingness to pay (WTP) from the mixed logit model by comparing the non cost part of utility with the cost coefficient. The non cost utility is defined as the sum of the programme constant and all attribute effects for the selected tier, career incentive, mentoring intensity, delivery mode and response time, leaving out the cost term.

Non cost utility
Non cost utility = Programme constant * Tier effect * Career incentive effect * Mentorship effect * Delivery mode effect * Response time effect

The cost coefficient is negative and reflects the marginal reduction in utility from a one unit increase in cost per 1 000 Rupees per trainee per month.

8.2 WTP per trainee per month

The willingness to pay per trainee per month in Rupees is calculated as the non cost utility divided by the absolute value of the cost coefficient, multiplied by 1 000 to return to Rupees.

WTP per trainee per month
WTP per trainee per month (Rupees) = [Non cost utility / |Cost coefficient|] × 1 000

This can be interpreted as the monthly amount per trainee that, according to the preference model, compensates stakeholders for the bundle of non cost features in the configuration. It is an indicative value measure, not a budget or price.

8.3 WTP per cohort and across cohorts

WTP per cohort aggregates the per trainee per month value over the programme duration and across all trainees in the cohort. Total WTP across cohorts multiplies the cohort level WTP by the number of cohorts.

WTP per cohort and national WTP
WTP per cohort = WTP per trainee per month × Duration in months × Trainees per cohort Total WTP across all cohorts = WTP per cohort × Number of cohorts

The results tab reports WTP per trainee per month and WTP per cohort. The national simulation tab reports total WTP across all cohorts as an indicative aggregate value.

9.3 Graduates and outbreak responses

For each cohort the expected number of graduates that are relevant for outbreak response is calculated as follows. First, the cohort size is multiplied by the completion rate to obtain the number of completers. Second, this is multiplied by the endorsement share, because only a fraction of potential deployments corresponds to the endorsed configuration. This gives the effective number of graduates per cohort. National totals multiply these values by the number of cohorts.

Graduates and outbreak responses
Effective graduates per cohort = Trainees per cohort × Completion rate × Endorsement share Outbreak responses per year per cohort = Effective graduates per cohort × Outbreaks per graduate per year × Response time multiplier

Outbreak responses per year at national level equal the per cohort figure multiplied by the number of cohorts.

9.4 Monetary outbreak benefits

Monetary outbreak benefit per year per cohort is calculated by multiplying outbreak responses per year per cohort by the value per outbreak. The tool then applies a present value factor to bring the stream of annual outbreak benefits over the planning horizon into a single present value.

Outbreak benefit per cohort
Annual outbreak benefit per cohort = Outbreak responses per year per cohort × Value per outbreak Present value factor = [1 − (1 + r)−T] / r Outbreak benefit per cohort (present value) = Annual outbreak benefit per cohort × Present value factor

Here r is the annual discount rate for epidemiological benefits and T is the planning horizon in years. In the default settings r is 0.03 and T is 5, but these can be changed in the settings tab.

In the current version the per graduate monetary benefit is set to zero, so the epidemiological benefit per cohort is entirely driven by outbreak responses.

10. Value per outbreak and discounting

The value per outbreak is a key driver of epidemiological benefits. To make it easier for users to explore plausible ranges, STEPS provides outbreak value presets and a flexible text parser for custom values. Presets include 4, 5, 10, 20, 30 and 40 million Rupees per outbreak. The parser recognises inputs like "20", "20m", "20 million", "2 cr", "0.02 bn" and direct Rupee values such as 40000000.

When a value is entered or a preset is selected, the tool converts the input into an internal Rupee amount. It then applies this value per outbreak consistently across all tiers and recomputes epidemiological benefits, benefit cost ratios and net benefits.

The discount rate for epidemiological benefits is set at three percent per year by default. The present value factor is calculated as the sum of discount factors for each year of the planning horizon. Users can adjust the discount rate and the planning horizon in the settings or advanced settings tab and see the impact on benefit cost ratios and net benefits.

11. Benefit cost ratios and net benefits

11.1 Cohort level ratios

The tool calculates benefit cost ratios and net benefits at the cohort level using outbreak related benefits and economic costs per cohort. The definitions are:

Benefit cost ratio and net benefit per cohort
``` Benefit cost ratio per cohort = Outbreak benefit per cohort / Total economic cost per cohort Net outbreak benefit per cohort = Outbreak benefit per cohort − Total economic cost per cohort

A benefit cost ratio above one and a positive net benefit indicate that, under the current assumptions, outbreak related monetary benefits exceed total economic costs at the cohort level.

11.2 National level ratios

National level summaries aggregate per cohort values across all cohorts. Total economic cost at national level is total economic cost per cohort multiplied by the number of cohorts. Total outbreak benefit at national level is outbreak benefit per cohort multiplied by the number of cohorts. National net benefit and national benefit cost ratios follow the same definitions as at cohort level.

National benefit cost ratio and net benefit
Total economic cost (all cohorts) = Total economic cost per cohort × Number of cohorts Total outbreak benefit (all cohorts) = Outbreak benefit per cohort × Number of cohorts National net benefit = Total outbreak benefit (all cohorts) − Total economic cost (all cohorts) National benefit cost ratio = Total outbreak benefit (all cohorts) / Total economic cost (all cohorts)

These values are shown in the national simulation tab and are also summarised in the headline briefing text under the configuration summary.

12. National simulation scaling

The national simulation tab summarises the scale of training and outbreak response implied by the chosen configuration and number of cohorts over the selected planning horizon. The main outputs are:

  • Total graduates across all cohorts, adjusted for completion rates and endorsement.
  • Outbreak responses per year at national level.
  • Total economic cost across cohorts.
  • Total outbreak benefit across cohorts.
  • National net benefit and benefit cost ratio.
  • Total willingness to pay across cohorts.

These outputs are also used to populate bar charts that compare total costs with total outbreak benefits and show total graduates and outbreak responses per year. The text on the national simulation tab provides a plain language interpretation of the numbers to assist in briefing discussions.

13. WTP based benefits and sensitivity analysis

The sensitivity analysis framework combines three elements: economic costs, outbreak related epidemiological benefits and willingness to pay from the preference study. The aim is to show how conclusions change under different outbreak values and under different ways of using WTP.

13.1 Internal construction of summary rows

For the current scenario and any saved scenarios, the tool constructs internal summary rows with the following components:

  • Total economic cost across all cohorts.
  • Total outbreak benefit across all cohorts.
  • Net outbreak benefit across all cohorts.
  • Benefit cost ratio based on outbreak benefits only.
  • Total WTP across all cohorts.
  • WTP component linked to outbreak response (30 percent of total WTP across cohorts).
  • Combined benefit equal to total WTP plus total outbreak benefit.
  • Net present value based on WTP only, defined as total WTP minus total economic cost.
  • Net present value based on combined benefits, defined as combined benefit minus total economic cost.

These internal rows form the basis of the sensitivity tables that are visible on the sensitivity tab.

13.2 Benefit definitions and endorsement adjustment

The sensitivity tab allows the user to choose between different benefit definitions through a simple dropdown. Two examples are:

  • WTP only, where only total WTP is counted as the benefit.
  • Endorsement adjusted WTP, where WTP is scaled by an endorsement rate (either the model implied rate or a user specified override).

The user can also choose whether outbreak benefits from the epidemiological module are included in combined benefits. If outbreak benefits are excluded, combined benefits reduce to WTP related benefits. If outbreak benefits are included, combined benefits reflect both WTP and outbreak benefits.

For each scenario the table displays total economic cost, net outbreak benefit, total WTP, the WTP share attributed to outbreak response, epidemiological outbreak benefits if included, endorsement rates, effective WTP under the chosen endorsement adjustment, and benefit cost ratios and net present values for WTP alone and for combined benefits.

13.3 Export and document ready summaries

The tool provides Excel and PDF export options for sensitivity summaries. Exports convert the formatted tables in the sensitivity tab into files that can be shared with ministries and partners. The PDF export uses a landscape A4 layout and automatically handles multi page tables when scenarios or columns are numerous.
```

```
This section illustrates the calculations step by step for one hypothetical configuration. For clarity, numbers are rounded to whole Rupees or to one decimal place where appropriate. In the application, internal calculations use full numerical precision.

14.1 Example configuration

Example scenario A

Suppose the user selects the following configuration:

  • Tier: Intermediate.
  • Career incentive: University qualification.
  • Mentorship: High.
  • Delivery mode: Blended.
  • Outbreak response time: 7 days (fixed).
  • Cost per trainee per month: 30 000 Rupees.
  • Trainees per cohort: 30.
  • Number of cohorts: 10.
  • Planning horizon: 5 years.
  • Epidemiological discount rate: 3 percent per year.
  • Value per outbreak: 4 000 000 Rupees.
  • Opportunity cost toggle: on.

14.2 Programme utility and endorsement

From Table 1, the relevant coefficients are:

  • Programme constant: 0.168.
  • Intermediate tier: 0.220.
  • University qualification: 0.017.
  • High mentorship: 0.640.
  • Delivery mode (blended): reference level, coefficient zero.
  • Response time 7 days: 0.610.
  • Cost coefficient: −0.005 per 1 000 Rupees.
  • Opt out constant: −0.601.

The cost per trainee per month is 30 000 Rupees, so in thousands this is 30. The cost term is −0.005 multiplied by 30, which equals −0.15. The non cost utility is the sum of the non cost terms:

  • 0.168 (programme constant)
  • plus 0.220 (intermediate tier)
  • plus 0.017 (university qualification)
  • plus 0.640 (high mentorship)
  • plus 0.000 (blended delivery)
  • plus 0.610 (7 days response)

The non cost utility is therefore 0.168 + 0.220 + 0.017 + 0.640 + 0 + 0.610 which equals 1.655. The programme utility adds the cost term:

  • Programme utility = 1.655 − 0.15 = 1.505.
  • Opt out utility = −0.601.

The endorsement probability is calculated as the exponential of the programme utility divided by the sum of exponentials of the programme and opt out utilities. Numerically this gives an endorsement probability of about 96 percent and an opt out probability of about 4 percent. In the results tab, these would be displayed as 96.0 percent and 4.0 percent respectively.

14.3 WTP per trainee and per cohort

WTP per trainee per month is calculated as the non cost utility divided by the absolute value of the cost coefficient, multiplied by 1 000:

  • Non cost utility = 1.655.
  • Absolute cost coefficient = 0.005.
  • Non cost utility divided by cost coefficient in absolute value is 1.655 divided by 0.005, which equals 331.
  • Multiplying by 1 000 gives 331 000 Rupees.

So WTP per trainee per month is about 331 000 Rupees. For an intermediate tier duration of twelve months and 30 trainees, WTP per cohort is:

  • WTP per cohort = 331 000 × 12 × 30.
  • That is 331 000 × 360, which equals 119 160 000 Rupees.

With ten cohorts, total WTP across cohorts is 1 191 600 000 Rupees.

14.4 Programme cost, opportunity cost and economic cost

The direct programme cost per cohort is calculated from the cost per trainee per month, duration and number of trainees:

  • Direct cost per cohort = 30 000 × 12 × 30 = 10 800 000 Rupees.

For the intermediate tier, the opportunity cost rate is 0.35. Therefore:

  • Opportunity cost per cohort = 10 800 000 × 0.35 = 3 780 000 Rupees.
  • Total economic cost per cohort = 10 800 000 + 3 780 000 = 14 580 000 Rupees.

With ten cohorts the total economic cost across cohorts is 145 800 000 Rupees.

14.5 Graduates and outbreak responses

For each cohort the expected number of graduates is 30 trainees multiplied by the completion rate of 0.9, which is 27. The endorsement share is about 0.96, so the effective graduates per cohort are:

  • Effective graduates per cohort ≈ 27 × 0.96 ≈ 25.9.

Each graduate is assumed to contribute to 0.5 outbreaks per year, and with a seven day response time the multiplier is 1.5. The expected outbreak responses per year per cohort are therefore:

  • Outbreak responses per year per cohort = 25.9 × 0.5 × 1.5.
  • This equals 25.9 × 0.75 which is about 19.4 outbreaks per year.

Across ten cohorts the outbreak responses per year at national level are about 194.

14.6 Outbreak benefits and benefit cost ratios

Annual outbreak benefit per cohort is the number of outbreak responses per year per cohort multiplied by the value per outbreak. With 19.4 outbreak responses per year and a value per outbreak of 4 000 000 Rupees, this is:

  • Annual outbreak benefit per cohort ≈ 19.4 × 4 000 000 = 77 600 000 Rupees.

With a five year planning horizon and a three percent discount rate, the present value factor is approximately 4.58. Outbreak benefit per cohort in present value terms is:

  • Outbreak benefit per cohort ≈ 77 600 000 × 4.58 ≈ 355 000 000 Rupees.

The benefit cost ratio per cohort is outbreak benefit per cohort divided by total economic cost per cohort:

  • Benefit cost ratio per cohort ≈ 355 000 000 / 14 580 000 ≈ 24.3.
  • Net outbreak benefit per cohort ≈ 355 000 000 − 14 580 000 ≈ 340 420 000 Rupees.

At national level, multiplying by ten cohorts gives a total outbreak benefit of around 3 550 000 000 Rupees and a total economic cost of 145 800 000 Rupees, with the same benefit cost ratio and total net benefit of around 3 404 200 000 Rupees.

14.7 Sensitivity and WTP combined metrics

In the sensitivity tab, this scenario appears with its total economic cost across cohorts, total outbreak benefit across cohorts, net outbreak benefit and outbreak based benefit cost ratio. Total WTP across cohorts of about 1.19 billion Rupees is also reported. If the tool attributes 30 percent of WTP to outbreak response, the WTP component linked to outbreak response is around 357 million Rupees.

Combined benefits, defined as total WTP plus total outbreak benefit, would be about 4.74 billion Rupees and the combined benefit cost ratio would be around 32.5. Net present value based on combined benefits would be combined benefits minus total economic cost, or around 4.6 billion Rupees.

15. Copilot interpretation and JSON export

The Copilot tab is designed to help users translate the numerical outputs of the STEPS tool into a narrative policy brief using Microsoft Copilot. The tab constructs an interpretation prompt that includes:

  • A text instruction that explains the role of FETP in India, the purpose of STEPS and the structure of the brief.
  • A description of expected sections such as background, scenario description, preference evidence and endorsement, economic costs, epidemiological effects, benefit cost results, distributional considerations and recommendations.
  • A structured JSON export that summarises the current scenario and its key outputs.

When the user presses the Copilot button, the tool builds a JSON object containing:

  • Metadata such as generation time, country, planning horizon and discount rate.
  • Configuration details including tier, career incentive, mentorship, delivery mode, response time, cost per trainee, trainees per cohort, number of cohorts and the opportunity cost setting.
  • Preference model outputs including endorsement and opt out rates, WTP per trainee per month, WTP per cohort and total WTP across cohorts.
  • Cost outputs including programme cost, opportunity cost, economic cost per cohort and total economic cost across cohorts.
  • Epidemiological outputs including graduates per cohort and total graduates, outbreak responses per year and outbreak benefits per cohort and across cohorts.
  • Benefit cost results including benefit cost ratios, net benefits and the WTP component linked to outbreak response.

The text prompt and JSON export are combined into a single block and placed in a text area. The tool then attempts to copy this text to the clipboard and opens Microsoft Copilot in a new browser tab. Users can paste the content directly into Copilot to generate a three to five page policy brief that is grounded entirely on the STEPS scenario JSON.

16. Assumptions and limitations

The STEPS FETP Scale Up Decision Aid is designed for scenario exploration, not forecasting. Key assumptions include:

  • The mixed logit coefficients used in the tool are stable and representative of current stakeholder preferences.
  • Cost templates and opportunity cost rates are appropriate for the tiers under consideration and remain valid over the planning horizon.
  • Completion rates, outbreaks per graduate per year and the response time multipliers capture the main epidemiological pathways from training to outbreak response.
  • The value per outbreak is a reasonable summary of avoided costs and health losses, even though actual outbreak impacts vary widely across settings and events.
  • Outbreak benefits are additive across cohorts and time and can be represented by a simple present value factor.
  • WTP values inferred from stated preference data can be interpreted as indicative monetary values that complement, but do not replace, budget data.

Users should treat outputs as inputs to structured discussion rather than definitive results. Sensitivity analysis, alternative outbreak values, alternative discount rates and adjustments to epidemiological parameters are important for robust policy dialogue. The Copilot tab is intended to support this dialogue by linking transparent assumptions and quantitative outputs to narrative briefings for ministries and partners.
```
