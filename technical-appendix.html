1. Introduction and purpose of the STEPS tool

The STEPS FETP Scale Up Decision Aid is an interactive tool that combines preference evidence, cost templates and outbreak related benefit assumptions to help design and assess Field Epidemiology Training Programme (FETP) scale up options in India.

The tool links four elements. First, a mixed logit model from a discrete choice experiment summarises stakeholder preferences over FETP package features and cost. Second, harmonised cost templates map a chosen monthly cost per trainee into a full programme cost for each tier. Third, simple epidemiological rules translate graduates into expected outbreak responses and monetary outbreak benefits over a user selected planning horizon. Fourth, the tool aggregates these pieces into willingness to pay (WTP), economic cost, benefit cost ratios, net benefits and national scale outputs, and provides structured exports for policy interpretation.

STEPS is scenario based. Users select a tier, career incentive, mentorship intensity, delivery mode, monthly cost per trainee, cohort size, number of cohorts, planning horizon and whether to include the opportunity cost of trainee time. The tool fixes outbreak response time at seven days and uses the mixed logit model to calculate endorsement and implied WTP. All outputs are deterministic functions of these inputs and of the default epidemiological settings, which can be adjusted in the settings and advanced settings tabs.

This appendix explains in plain language how the calculations are implemented in the current version of the script, without requiring the reader to inspect the code. Where appropriate, a single short expression is given but the focus is on clear verbal descriptions and worked examples.

2. Preference study and mixed logit specification

2.1 Discrete choice experiment

Preferences for FETP scale up options were elicited using a discrete choice experiment (DCE). Respondents were presented with paired programme options and an opt out alternative in which no additional FETP training would be funded. Each programme differed along a set of attributes that reflect key design decisions that ministries and partners face when scaling up FETP.

Tier of training Frontline, Intermediate, Advanced.

Mentorship intensity Low, Medium, High.

Delivery mode Blended, Fully in person, Fully online.

Career incentive Government and partner certificate, University qualification, Government career pathway recognition.

Outbreak response time 30 days, 15 days, 7 days to detect and respond to priority outbreaks.

Cost per trainee per month Several levels in Indian Rupees.

Attribute levels were chosen through expert consultation and piloting to represent realistic policy options. Cost levels were selected so that the mixed logit model could recover a stable cost coefficient and implied WTP values.

2.2 Mixed logit structure used in STEPS

The DCE data were analysed using a mixed logit model. In the original estimation, coefficients were allowed to vary across respondents to capture preference heterogeneity. For the STEPS tool, the mean preference estimates from that mixed logit model are used as fixed coefficients to build utilities and probabilities.

For the current version of the tool, utilities depend on:

  • an alternative specific constant for programme options, labelled ascProgram
  • an alternative specific constant for the opt out option, labelled ascOptOut
  • tier effects for intermediate and advanced tiers, relative to frontline
  • career incentive effects for university qualification and career pathway, relative to certificate
  • mentorship effects for medium and high mentoring, relative to low
  • delivery effects for fully in person and fully online, relative to blended
  • response time effects for 15 days and 7 days, relative to 30 days
  • a cost coefficient expressed per 1 000 Rupees per trainee per month.

3. Programme attributes and fixed 7 day response time

In the current version of the tool, users can choose tier, career incentive, mentorship intensity, delivery mode, monthly cost per trainee, cohort size and number of cohorts. Outbreak response time is fixed to seven days in the configuration. The response dropdown is present in the interface but is automatically set to seven days and other values are disabled and over-written when changed.

Internally, the tool records the following configuration elements:

  • Tier: frontline, intermediate or advanced.
  • Career incentive: certificate, uniqual or career_path.
  • Mentorship intensity: low, medium or high.
  • Delivery mode: blended, inperson or online.
  • Response time: fixed to "7" days.
  • Cost per trainee per month: numeric value in Rupees.
  • Trainees per cohort, Number of cohorts, Planning horizon in years.
  • Opportunity cost included or excluded through a toggle switch.

The fixed seven day response time affects both the preference model, through the positive coefficient for seven days relative to 30 days, and the epidemiological module, through an outbreak response time multiplier (1.5 for seven days) used when computing outbreak responses and benefits.

4. Cost templates and cost construction

STEPS uses harmonised cost templates for each tier to translate a user supplied monthly cost per trainee into a full programme cost for a cohort and an economic cost that can include the opportunity cost of trainee time. These templates are defined in the COST_TEMPLATES object in the script.

For each tier there is a single combined template that aggregates across institutions and includes both direct cost shares for different components and a tier specific opportunity cost rate. The combined template for each tier is summarised in Table 2.

Within each template, the direct cost shares of individual components are also specified. These shares are used to provide a breakdown of programme cost into staff, travel, workshops and other items, and to display approximate per trainee per month values for each component in the costing tab. The direct cost shares in the script sum to one within rounding for each tier.

5. From cost per trainee to economic cost per cohort

The user sets a monthly cost per trainee in Rupees. STEPS converts this into programme cost per cohort and total economic cost per cohort. The steps are as follows.

5.1 Programme duration

The tool uses fixed durations by tier, expressed in months:

  • Frontline: 3 months
  • Intermediate: 12 months
  • Advanced: 24 months

5.2 Programme cost per cohort

Programme cost per cohort is the direct financial cost of running one cohort and is calculated as the monthly cost per trainee multiplied by the duration in months and the number of trainees per cohort.

Programme cost per cohort

Programme cost per cohort = monthly cost per trainee × months in tier × trainees per cohort.

5.3 Opportunity cost and economic cost per cohort

When the opportunity cost toggle is on, STEPS multiplies programme cost per cohort by the tier specific opportunity cost rate to obtain the opportunity cost of trainee time. This value is then added to programme cost to define total economic cost per cohort. When the toggle is off, the opportunity cost rate is treated as zero and total economic cost equals the programme cost.

Total economic cost per cohort

Total economic cost per cohort = programme cost per cohort + opportunity cost per cohort, where opportunity cost per cohort = programme cost per cohort × opportunity cost rate when the toggle is on and zero otherwise.

The costing tab displays programme cost, opportunity cost, total economic cost and the share of economic cost that is accounted for by opportunity cost. It also displays a cost breakdown by major component, based on the direct cost shares in the cost template and the chosen programme cost per cohort.

6. Willingness to pay calculations

STEPS reports WTP per trainee per month, WTP per cohort and total WTP across all cohorts implied by the preference model. These are not budget amounts. They are indicative values in Rupees derived from stakeholder stated preferences.

6.1 Utility for the programme option and opt out

For a given configuration, the tool constructs the utility of the programme option by summing:

  • the programme constant ascProgram
  • the tier effect for intermediate or advanced if applicable
  • the career effect for university qualification or career path if applicable
  • the mentorship effect for medium or high mentoring if applicable
  • the delivery effect for in person or online delivery if applicable
  • the response time effect for seven days (relative to 30 days)
  • the cost term, which is the cost coefficient multiplied by cost expressed in thousands of Rupees per trainee per month.

The utility of the opt out option is simply the opt out constant ascOptOut.

6.2 Endorsement and opt out rates

STEPS converts the two utilities into probabilities using the standard two option logit formula. It first subtracts the maximum of the two utilities from each to avoid numerical overflow, exponentiates the adjusted utilities, and divides the exponentiated programme utility by the sum of both exponentiated utilities. This yields the predicted probability that respondents choose the programme option when confronted with the opt out alternative. The endorsement rate is this probability expressed as a percentage. The opt out rate is one hundred minus the endorsement rate.

6.3 WTP per trainee per month and per cohort

To obtain WTP per trainee per month, STEPS uses the non cost part of the programme utility, that is, the sum of all relevant coefficients for the programme option except the cost coefficient. This non cost utility is divided by the absolute value of the cost coefficient, which is expressed per 1 000 Rupees. Multiplying by 1 000 then converts from thousands of Rupees to Rupees per trainee per month.

WTP definitions in STEPS

Non cost utility is the sum of the programme constant and all attribute level coefficients for the chosen tier, career incentive, mentorship, delivery and response time, excluding cost. WTP per trainee per month is this non cost utility divided by the absolute value of the cost coefficient and multiplied by one thousand. WTP per cohort is WTP per trainee per month multiplied by trainees per cohort and the number of months in the tier. Total WTP across all cohorts is WTP per cohort multiplied by the number of cohorts.

The sensitivity tab also uses a simple rule to split total WTP into a general value component and an outbreak related component, with the outbreak component set to 30 percent of total WTP across cohorts in the current script. This is labelled the WTP outbreak component in the tables.

7. Epidemiological benefit calculations

Epidemiological benefits in STEPS are based on simple rules for graduates, outbreak responses and the monetary value assigned to each outbreak response. The current default settings are defined in the DEFAULT_EPI_SETTINGS object and can be edited in the settings and advanced settings tabs.

7.1 Core epidemiological settings

The general settings include:

  • a planning horizon in years (default 5 years)
  • a discount rate for epidemiological benefits (default 3 percent per year)
  • a Rupee to US dollar exchange rate for display conversions only (default 83 INR per USD).

Tier specific settings for frontline, intermediate and advanced are identical by default:

  • completion rate: 0.9 (90 percent of enrolled trainees complete)
  • outbreaks handled per graduate per year: 0.5
  • value per graduate: 0 (not used in this version)
  • value per outbreak: 4 000 000 INR by default, but adjustable through settings and sensitivity controls.

7.2 Present value factor

The tool summarises future outbreak related benefits over the planning horizon using a standard present value factor for a constant annual benefit. For a positive discount rate, the factor is equal to the sum of one divided by one plus the discount rate, taken over each year of the planning horizon. Numerically, this is implemented using a compact expression in the script but conceptually it is the sum of discounted annual benefits over the specified years.

7.3 Graduates and outbreak responses

For a given configuration, STEPS first calculates the number of trainees per cohort and adjusts this using the completion rate to obtain the number of graduates per cohort. It then adjusts graduates by the endorsement rate, expressed as a proportion rather than a percentage, to obtain the number of effective graduates per cohort. This reflects the assumption that scale up is proportional to the share of stakeholders who endorse the configuration.

The number of graduates across all cohorts is the effective graduates per cohort multiplied by the number of cohorts. The expected number of outbreak responses per year per cohort is given by effective graduates per cohort multiplied by the assumed number of outbreaks handled per graduate per year and multiplied by a response time multiplier. The response time multiplier is one for 30 days, 1.2 for 15 days and 1.5 for seven days. Because the current tool fixes response time at seven days, the multiplier is always 1.5.

Outbreak responses per year at national level are computed by multiplying the per cohort outbreak responses per year by the number of cohorts.

7.4 Outbreak related benefits

The annual outbreak benefit per cohort is the product of the number of outbreak responses per year per cohort and the monetary value per outbreak. The present value of this annual benefit stream over the planning horizon is then the annual benefit multiplied by the present value factor described above. In the current script, graduate benefits are set to zero and only this outbreak related component contributes to epidemiological benefits.

Epidemiological benefit per cohort

Epidemiological benefit per cohort is equal to the present value factor multiplied by outbreak responses per year per cohort and the value per outbreak. Total epidemiological benefit across all cohorts multiplies the per cohort benefit by the number of cohorts.

8. Benefit cost ratios, net benefits and national scaling

With total economic cost per cohort and epidemiological benefit per cohort defined, STEPS constructs benefit cost ratios and net benefits at cohort and national levels. It also aggregates WTP across cohorts and presents these outputs in the results and national tabs.

8.1 Cohort level indicators

At cohort level, the key indicators are:

  • Epidemiological benefit per cohort, as described in Section 7.
  • Total economic cost per cohort, including opportunity cost when this is switched on.
  • Net outbreak benefit per cohort, defined as epidemiological benefit per cohort minus total economic cost per cohort.
  • Benefit cost ratio per cohort, defined as epidemiological benefit per cohort divided by total economic cost per cohort, when total economic cost is positive.

8.2 National level indicators

National level indicators are built by multiplying cohort level quantities by the number of cohorts. Specifically:

  • Total economic cost across all cohorts is total economic cost per cohort multiplied by the number of cohorts.
  • Total epidemiological outbreak benefit across all cohorts is epidemiological benefit per cohort multiplied by the number of cohorts.
  • National net outbreak benefit is total outbreak benefit minus total economic cost.
  • National benefit cost ratio is total outbreak benefit divided by total economic cost when total economic cost is positive.
  • Total graduates and outbreak responses per year are aggregated similarly.
  • Total WTP across all cohorts is WTP per cohort multiplied by the number of cohorts.

The national tab presents these indicators and a narrative summary that explains costs, outbreak related economic cost savings, benefit cost ratios and net benefits in Rupees. Charts display total cost versus total outbreak savings and total graduates versus outbreak responses per year for the configured scenario.

9. Sensitivity analysis and benefit definitions

The sensitivity tab shows how total cost, WTP, outbreak related benefits and net benefits change across different scenarios and benefit definitions. Users can alter the value per outbreak, decide whether to include outbreak benefits in benefit cost ratios and net benefits, and optionally override the endorsement rate used in some calculations.

9.1 Value per outbreak sensitivity

A set of outbreak value presets, expressed in millions of Rupees, is available in dropdowns on the settings and sensitivity interfaces. When a user selects and applies a preset, the script converts the selected value into Rupees and assigns it as the value per outbreak for all tiers. The figures displayed in the epidemiological and benefit cost calculations then update accordingly.

9.2 Benefit modes

The script supports different ways of defining benefits in the sensitivity tables. The main elements are:

  • Total WTP across all cohorts from the preference model.
  • WTP outbreak component, equal to 30 percent of total WTP across all cohorts.
  • Epidemiological outbreak benefits from the epidemiological module.
  • Effective WTP, optionally scaled by an endorsement override value if set.

The sensitivity tables report cost, net benefit and benefit cost ratios under different benefit definitions, such as WTP only, WTP plus outbreak benefits, and endorsement adjusted WTP. These calculations are purely algebraic rearrangements of the basic outputs described above.

10. Worked example of all calculations

This section walks through a complete example using the current script logic. The numerical values are illustrative but follow the exact calculation steps used by the tool.

10.1 Scenario description

Example scenario

Tier: Intermediate (tier = "intermediate").

Career incentive: University qualification (career = "uniqual").

Mentorship intensity: High (mentorship = "high").

Delivery mode: Blended (delivery = "blended").

Response time: Fixed at 7 days (response = "7" enforced by the script).

Cost per trainee per month: 50 000 INR.

Trainees per cohort: 25.

Number of cohorts: 4.

Planning horizon: 5 years.

Discount rate for epidemiological benefits: 3 percent per year.

Opportunity cost: Included.

10.2 Programme utility, endorsement and WTP

For this configuration, STEPS calculates programme utility by summing the relevant coefficients. The selected levels are intermediate tier, university qualification, high mentoring, blended delivery and seven day response time. Cost per trainee per month is 50 000 INR, which is treated as 50 in thousands.

The non cost part of the programme utility is the sum of:

  • ascProgram = 0.168
  • Tier: intermediate = 0.220
  • Career: university qualification = 0.017
  • Mentorship: high = 0.640
  • Delivery: blended = 0 (reference category)
  • Response: 7 days = 0.610

Adding these gives a non cost utility of 1.655.

The cost term is cost per thousand multiplied by the cost coefficient. Cost per thousand is 50 000 divided by 1 000, which equals 50. The cost coefficient is -0.005. The cost term is therefore 50 × (-0.005) = -0.25. Programme utility is the sum of non cost utility and the cost term, so 1.655 - 0.25 = 1.405.

The utility of the opt out option is the opt out constant, ascOptOut = -0.601. To convert these utilities to probabilities, the tool subtracts the maximum of the two utilities from each, exponentiates the adjusted utilities, and divides the exponentiated programme utility by the sum of both exponentiated utilities. In this example the programme utility is higher, so it is used as the baseline. The opt out utility is further from zero after the adjustment and its exponential is smaller. The resulting endorsement probability is then multiplied by one hundred to obtain the endorsement rate in percent, and the opt out rate is one hundred minus this figure.

WTP per trainee per month is computed by dividing the non cost utility of 1.655 by the absolute value of the cost coefficient, which is 0.005, and multiplying by 1 000. The ratio 1.655 ÷ 0.005 equals 331. Multiplying by 1 000 gives a WTP per trainee per month of 331 000 INR.

WTP per cohort multiplies this WTP per trainee per month by the number of months in the intermediate tier and the number of trainees per cohort. The intermediate tier has 12 months, so WTP per cohort is 331 000 × 12 × 25. WTP across all cohorts then multiplies this value by the number of cohorts, which is four.

10.3 Programme and economic cost

Programme cost per cohort is monthly cost per trainee multiplied by duration and trainees. With 50 000 INR per trainee per month, 12 months and 25 trainees, programme cost per cohort is 50 000 × 12 × 25 = 15 000 000 INR.

Because opportunity cost is included, the tool applies the intermediate tier opportunity cost rate of 0.35 to programme cost per cohort. The opportunity cost per cohort is 0.35 × 15 000 000 = 5 250 000 INR. Total economic cost per cohort is the sum of programme cost and opportunity cost, which is 20 250 000 INR.

Economic cost across all cohorts is total economic cost per cohort multiplied by the number of cohorts, so 20 250 000 × 4 = 81 000 000 INR.

10.4 Graduates, outbreak responses and outbreak benefits

The intermediate tier completion rate is 0.9, meaning 90 percent of trainees complete the programme. With 25 trainees per cohort, this yields 22.5 graduates per cohort. These are then adjusted by the endorsement rate, expressed as a proportion, to obtain effective graduates per cohort. For example, if the endorsement rate were 70 percent, the effective graduates per cohort would be 22.5 × 0.70 = 15.75.

Graduates across all cohorts multiply this number by the four cohorts. Outbreak responses per year per cohort are given by effective graduates per cohort multiplied by the outbreaks per graduate per year (0.5 by default) and the response time multiplier of 1.5 for seven day response time. Outbreak responses per year at national level multiply this figure by the number of cohorts.

Annual outbreak benefit per cohort equals outbreak responses per year per cohort multiplied by the value per outbreak. With a default value per outbreak of 4 000 000 INR, the annual benefit is 4 000 000 times outbreak responses per year per cohort. The present value factor for a five year horizon and a three percent discount rate is applied to this annual benefit to obtain the outbreak related benefit per cohort over the planning horizon. Total outbreak benefit across all cohorts multiplies this per cohort figure by the number of cohorts.

10.5 Benefit cost ratios, net benefits and WTP decomposition

Cohort level benefit cost ratio is outbreak benefit per cohort divided by total economic cost per cohort. Net benefit per cohort is outbreak benefit per cohort minus total economic cost per cohort. At national level, the benefit cost ratio and net benefit are computed in the same way using total outbreak benefits and total economic costs across all cohorts.

The sensitivity tab reports total WTP across all cohorts, the WTP outbreak component defined as 30 percent of total WTP, epidemiological outbreak benefits when included, and combinations of these. It also reports net benefits and benefit cost ratios for these different benefit definitions and allows endorsement override values to be applied when desired.

11. Copilot interpretation prompt and JSON export

The Copilot tab in STEPS prepares a structured prompt that combines a narrative instruction and a full JSON export of the current scenario. The aim is to help users generate a draft three to five page policy brief in Microsoft Copilot that interprets the STEPS outputs for senior decision makers.

11.1 Copilot interpretation text

The interpretation text describes the role of FETP, the purpose of STEPS and the desired structure of the brief. It guides Copilot to discuss background, scenario description, preference study results and endorsement, economic costs, epidemiological effects, benefit cost results, distributional and implementation considerations and recommendations. It also invites Copilot to use compact tables when helpful while keeping the narrative readable.

11.2 Scenario JSON structure

The script builds a JSON object for the current scenario that contains:

  • Metadata: tool name, country, generation time, planning horizon and discount rate.
  • Configuration: name, notes, tier, career incentive, mentorship, delivery mode, response time, cost per trainee per month, trainees per cohort, number of cohorts and whether opportunity cost is included.
  • Preference model outputs: model type, endorsement and opt out rates, WTP per trainee per month, WTP per cohort and total WTP across cohorts.
  • Cost results: programme cost per cohort, opportunity cost per cohort, total economic cost per cohort and total economic cost across cohorts.
  • Epidemiological results: graduates per cohort and across cohorts, outbreak responses per year per cohort and at national level, epidemiological benefits per cohort and across cohorts.
  • Benefit cost results: cohort and national benefit cost ratios, net benefits per cohort and across cohorts, the WTP outbreak component and total WTP.

The Copilot button copies a combined text that includes the interpretation instructions and the JSON between clear markers. It also opens Microsoft Copilot in a new browser tab. Users paste the copied text into Copilot, run the request and review the resulting brief for consistency with the STEPS outputs and for local adaptation.

12. Summary of assumptions and limitations

The current version of STEPS is a structured calculator built on transparent assumptions that can be changed by users but are not inferred from data within the tool. Key assumptions include:

  • mean coefficients from a mixed logit model are used as fixed parameters for endorsement and WTP calculations
  • response time is fixed at seven days in the configuration stage even though the original DCE distinguished between 30, 15 and seven days
  • programme duration by tier and completion rates are fixed values but can be edited through settings
  • the relationship between graduates and outbreak responses is linear and does not model complex epidemiological dynamics
  • value per outbreak is assumed and can be varied through sensitivity analysis, but is not estimated within the tool
  • opportunity cost of trainee time is applied as a simple proportional mark up to programme cost.

All calculations in this appendix reflect the current implementation of the script provided. Any future updates to the preference model, cost templates, epidemiological settings or Copilot prompt would require corresponding updates to this appendix to maintain alignment between documentation and the underlying code.
