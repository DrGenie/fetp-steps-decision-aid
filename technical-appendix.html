Technical Appendix – Cost and Benefit Calculations in the STEPS FETP India Tool

This appendix explains how the STEPS FETP India decision aid converts discrete choice experiment (DCE) results into predicted endorsement, willingness to pay (WTP), monetary benefits and benefit cost ratios for specific programme scenarios. All calculations in this appendix use:

  • 50 trainees per cohort;
  • 10 cohorts in total (that is 500 trainees altogether);
  • a constant annual discount rate of 3 percent;
  • a five year time horizon for benefits and costs.

Monetary values are expressed in Indian rupees. The cost attribute in the DCE is measured as cost per trainee per month in thousands of rupees (₹000).

1. Mixed logit model used in the STEPS tool

The STEPS tool is based on a mixed logit (MXL) model estimated from the stakeholder discrete choice experiment. The model produces a utility score for each training programme and for the opt out option. The key mean coefficients used in the tool are:

Component Level Coefficient Notes
Alternative specific constant Any FETP programme 0.168 Baseline preference for joining a programme rather than opting out
Alternative specific constant Opt out -0.601 Preference for not joining any programme
Programme type Frontline 0.000 Reference level
Programme type Intermediate 0.220 Extra value for an intermediate programme
Programme type Advanced 0.487 Extra value for an advanced programme
Mentoring intensity Low 0.000 Reference level
Mentoring intensity Medium 0.453 Extra value of medium mentoring
Mentoring intensity High 0.640 Extra value of high mentoring
Delivery mode Blended 0.000 Reference level
Delivery mode In person -0.232 Penalty on utility for fully in person delivery
Delivery mode Online -1.073 Large penalty for fully online delivery
Trainee career incentive Government and partner certificate 0.000 Reference level
Trainee career incentive University qualification 0.017 Slight extra utility for a formal university qualification
Outbreak response capacity Within 30 days 0.000 Reference level
Outbreak response capacity Within 15 days 0.546 Extra value for a 15 day response capacity
Outbreak response capacity Within 7 days 0.610 Extra value for a 7 day response capacity
Cost per trainee per month Per ₹1000 -0.005 Negative coefficient for higher monthly cost

For any scenario the tool first calculates a utility score that ignores cost (the non cost utility) by adding the relevant coefficients from the table above. It then subtracts the cost effect, which is the cost coefficient multiplied by the cost level in thousands of rupees per month.

2. From utility scores to endorsement probabilities

The probability that stakeholders choose a particular programme instead of opting out is calculated using the standard logit formula. The tool compares the utility for the programme with the utility for the opt out option:

Example for Scenario S5 (Intermediate, Medium mentoring, Blended delivery, Government and partner certificate, response within 7 days, cost ₹75,000 per trainee per month):

  1. Non cost utility for S5 is calculated by adding the relevant coefficients:
    0.168 (programme constant) + 0.220 (intermediate) + 0.453 (medium mentoring) + 0.000 (blended delivery) + 0.000 (certificate) + 0.610 (response within 7 days) = 1.451.
  2. The cost level is 75 on the ₹000 scale. The cost effect is -0.005 × 75 = -0.375. The full programme utility for S5 is therefore 1.451 - 0.375 = 1.076.
  3. The utility for the opt out option is the constant only, which is -0.601.
  4. The endorsement probability is then the exponential of 1.076 divided by the sum of the exponentials of 1.076 and -0.601. This gives a predicted endorsement rate for S5 of about 84.3 percent.

The same procedure is applied in the tool for each scenario and for any user defined configuration of the attributes.

3. From utility scores to willingness to pay

The DCE also provides a way to express the overall value of a programme in monetary terms. The cost coefficient tells us how utility changes when cost increases by ₹1000 per trainee per month. Because the coefficient is -0.005, a cost increase of ₹1000 reduces utility by 0.005 units.

For a given programme, the non cost utility represents the extra utility relative to a reference programme at the same cost. The willingness to pay per trainee per month that would compensate for this difference is obtained by dividing the non cost utility by the absolute value of the cost coefficient and then multiplying by 1000 to convert back to rupees:

WTP per trainee per month = (non cost utility ÷ 0.005) × 1000.

For Scenario S5 the non cost utility is 1.451. The implied willingness to pay per trainee per month is:

(1.451 ÷ 0.005) × 1000 = 290,200 rupees per trainee per month.

This means stakeholders value the combination of intermediate training, medium mentoring, blended delivery, a government and partner certificate and a seven day outbreak response capacity at about ₹290,200 per trainee per month compared with the reference programme.

4. Aggregating WTP to benefits for 50 trainees and 10 cohorts

The STEPS tool aggregates willingness to pay across trainees and cohorts to obtain annual monetary benefits for a selected configuration. The steps are:

  1. Multiply the WTP per trainee per month by the number of trainees in one cohort. With 50 trainees per cohort and S5 WTP of ₹290,200 per trainee per month, the benefit per cohort per month is:
    290,200 × 50 = 14,510,000 rupees.
  2. Multiply by the number of cohorts. With 10 cohorts the total monthly benefit is:
    14,510,000 × 10 = 145,100,000 rupees.
  3. Multiply by 12 to obtain annual benefits. For S5 the annual WTP based benefit across 10 cohorts is:
    145,100,000 × 12 = 1,741,200,000 rupees (that is 1,741.2 million rupees).

The STEPS tool repeats this calculation automatically for any scenario chosen in the configuration tab, using the current cohort size and number of cohorts entered by the user.

5. Discounting and the present value factor

To compare costs and benefits over time the tool converts the future annual benefits and costs into a five year present value using a constant discount rate of 3 percent. If the annual benefit is the same in each year, the present value can be written as the annual benefit multiplied by a present value factor.

For a five year horizon and a 3 percent discount rate, the discount factor for each year is:

Year Discount factor 1 ÷ (1.03)t
1 0.9708737864
2 0.9425959091
3 0.9151416559
4 0.8884957882
5 0.8626201845

Adding these five factors gives the present value factor:

0.9708737864 + 0.9425959091 + 0.9151416559 + 0.8884957882 + 0.8626201845 = 4.5797071872 (more precisely 4.579707187194534).

The present value of a constant annual benefit is therefore:

Present value of benefits = annual benefit × 4.5797071872.

For Scenario S5 the annual WTP based benefit is 1,741.2 million rupees. Multiplying by the present value factor gives:

1,741.2 × 4.5797071872 = 7,974.2 million rupees (approximately).

6. Programme costs for 50 trainees and 10 cohorts

The cost attribute in the DCE is the direct programme cost per trainee per month measured in thousands of rupees. For each scenario the STEPS tool converts this into annual programme costs for the chosen cohort size and number of cohorts.

The direct training cost per month for one cohort is the monthly cost per trainee multiplied by the number of trainees in the cohort. For Scenario S5 this gives:

Monthly cost per trainee = 75,000 rupees.
Trainees per cohort = 50.
Monthly direct cost per cohort = 75,000 × 50 = 3,750,000 rupees.

With 10 cohorts the monthly direct training cost is:

3,750,000 × 10 = 37,500,000 rupees.

Multiplying by 12 gives the annual direct cost:

37,500,000 × 12 = 450,000,000 rupees (450 million rupees per year).

Applying the same five year present value factor of 4.5797071872 gives the five year present value of direct costs:

450 × 4.5797071872 = 2,060.9 million rupees.

The STEPS tool can also include opportunity costs of trainee time. Users enter the monthly opportunity cost per trainee in the configuration tab. The tool then adds this to the direct programme cost using exactly the same structure as above (opportunity cost per trainee per month × trainees per cohort × number of cohorts × 12 months, then multiplied by the present value factor).

7. Benefit cost ratio for Scenario S5

For Scenario S5, bringing together the present value of benefits and the present value of direct programme costs for 10 cohorts of 50 trainees gives:

  • Present value of WTP based benefits: 7,974.2 million rupees.
  • Present value of direct programme costs: 2,060.9 million rupees.

The benefit cost ratio (BCR) is the ratio of these two figures:

BCR = 7,974.2 ÷ 2,060.9 ≈ 3.87.

In other words, under the assumptions used in the STEPS tool, each rupee of direct programme cost in Scenario S5 is associated with about 3.9 rupees of stakeholder willingness to pay for the programme benefits over the five year horizon, when training 10 cohorts of 50 trainees each.
