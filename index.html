What STEPS does

STEPS helps policy makers and partners explore options for scaling Field Epidemiology Training Programmes in India. It links three main inputs:

  • Preferences and willingness-to-pay estimates from a discrete choice experiment with stakeholders.
  • Costing templates from WHO, NIE and NCDC for Frontline, Intermediate and Advanced programmes.
  • Simple epidemiological multipliers that translate training scale-up and outbreak response times into health benefits.

Users can set a programme configuration, see endorsement rates, willingness to pay, costs and benefit cost ratios, and compare saved scenarios side by side for business case discussions.

The tool is designed to support transparent conversations with the Government of India and other partners. It keeps all assumptions visible and connects preference evidence directly to programme design, economic summaries and epidemiological implications.

How to use the tool

  1. Go to the Configuration tab and choose programme tier, career incentive, mentorship intensity, response time, delivery mode, number of cohorts, cohort size and cost per trainee per month.
  2. Keep the cost slider within the range used in the experiment (75,000 to 400,000 rupees per trainee per month).
  3. Select the preference model, which uses the average mixed logit estimates from the discrete choice experiment, and leave it on this default setting for policy discussions.
  4. Click Apply configuration to update all calculations, then use View results summary to open a scenario summary with headline recommendations.
  5. Save promising scenarios in the Saved scenarios tab and download them as Excel or a policy brief PDF for reporting and World Bank documentation.
  6. For workshops or sensitivity checks, use the National simulation and Sensitivity / DCE benefits tabs to explore how results change with cohorts, endorsement and different benefit definitions, and the Advanced and methods tab to adjust multipliers and open the full technical appendix.

STEPS is designed for laptops, desktops, tablets and mobiles.

Attributes and levels in the FETP preference study

The discrete choice experiment asked stakeholders to choose between alternative FETP programme designs and an opt-out option. Each programme was described by the attributes below. STEPS keeps programme configurations inside the experimental range so that results remain consistent with the underlying models.

Opt-out and cost templates

Element Definition How STEPS uses it
Opt-out alternative
An opt-out option where no new FETP training is funded under the scenario being considered.
The discrete choice experiment included an explicit opt-out option. STEPS uses this as the benchmark when calculating endorsement. Endorsement is the predicted share choosing the programme configuration rather than opting out.
Cost templates
Template budgets submitted by WHO, NIE and NCDC for different FETP tiers.
Templates are used as alternative settings rather than being combined. Policy makers can switch between templates to see how cost structure affects economic cost while holding programme design constant. Templates that include opportunity cost feed into total economic cost when the opportunity cost switch is on.
Opportunity cost of trainee time
The value of trainee salary time spent in training instead of normal duties, per trainee per month.
When the opportunity cost toggle is on, STEPS adds this to programme cost, yielding total economic cost. When off, outputs focus on budgetary cost only.
Preference models
Mixed logit model estimated on the full stakeholder sample.
The tool applies the average mixed logit model from the discrete choice experiment to predict endorsement and derive willingness-to-pay values. This maintains a single coherent preference structure for all scenarios.

Training B and reference attribute levels form the baseline. The alternative specific constant for Training A captures a layout effect. The opt-out constant captures the general tendency to choose neither training option. STEPS uses the opt-out constant when predicting overall endorsement of any FETP option versus no FETP.

Endorsement and opt-out

These percentages are obtained by plugging the chosen configuration into the mixed logit model and comparing the utility of any FETP option with the utility of opting out. They summarise how attractive the configuration is to stakeholders in the preference study.

Endorse FETP option -

Estimated share of stakeholders who would support funding this programme rather than keeping the status quo.

Choose opt-out -

Estimated share who would prefer not to invest in this option under the assumptions in the study.

Willingness to pay and economic outcomes

Willingness to pay (WTP) per trainee per month is derived from the preference model by dividing attribute coefficients by the cost coefficient. It indicates how much value stakeholders attach to the bundle of attributes in this configuration relative to a baseline. Total WTP aggregates this value across all trainees for each cohort and at national scale.

WTP per trainee per month -

Indicative value stakeholders place on this configuration per trainee per month, relative to the baseline design used in the experiment.

Total WTP per cohort -

WTP per trainee per month multiplied by trainees per cohort and programme duration, treating stakeholder preferences as an additional benefit measure.

Programme cost per cohort -

Direct programme cost per cohort before adding any opportunity cost of trainee time.

Total economic cost per cohort -

Programme cost plus opportunity cost where applicable. This is the main cost figure used in benefit cost ratios.

Net benefit per cohort (epidemiological) -

Indicative epidemiological benefit per cohort minus total economic cost per cohort, combining graduate and outbreak response benefits. WTP is reported separately as a preference-based benefit metric.

Benefit cost ratio (per cohort) -

Total indicative epidemiological benefit per cohort divided by total economic cost per cohort. Values above one suggest that epidemiological benefits exceed costs on the assumptions used.

Epidemiological implications

Graduates and outbreak responses are obtained by combining endorsement with simple multipliers on cohort size and number of cohorts. Indicative benefit per cohort converts additional graduates and outbreak responses into monetary terms using values that can be adjusted in the Advanced and methods tab. The expected response time attribute from the discrete choice experiment is used to interpret how quickly these benefits accrue and to structure discussions about the value of faster detection.

FETP graduates (all cohorts) -

Estimated number of graduates produced across all planned cohorts, adjusting for endorsement and completion rates.

Outbreak responses per year -

Approximate number of outbreak responses that could be supported per year once all graduates are in post under this configuration.

Indicative epidemiological benefit per cohort -

Monetary value of graduates and outbreak responses per cohort using the values specified in the Advanced and methods tab or epidemiological configuration file.

Cost templates and sources

Cost templates are based on submissions from WHO, NIE and NCDC. For Frontline training, WHO supplies a template for six cohorts. For Intermediate training, templates are available from WHO, NIE and NCDC. For Advanced training, templates are available from NIE and NCDC.

Templates are used as alternative settings rather than being combined. Policy makers can switch between templates to see how cost structure affects economic cost while holding programme design constant.

Templates that include the opportunity cost of trainee time feed into total economic cost when the opportunity cost switch is on. When it is off, results focus on budgetary costs only.

Sensitivity and DCE benefit summary

This tab brings together preference-based benefits from the discrete choice experiment and the cost and epidemiological modules. It shows the total willingness-to-pay benefit associated with each configuration, the share that is specifically linked to faster outbreak response, any additional outbreak-related benefits from the epidemiological module when activated, and summary cost benefit indicators such as the benefit cost ratio and net present value for each scenario.

This view turns the discrete choice experiment estimates, cost templates and epidemiological multipliers into a compact sensitivity table. Each row represents a scenario, starting with the current configuration and followed by any saved scenarios. For each scenario, STEPS recomputes endorsement, economic cost per cohort, willingness-to-pay based benefits, outbreak-response contributions, epidemiological outbreak benefits and combined cost benefit metrics using the selected preference model and opportunity cost setting.

Use this table to compare how different benefit definitions perform relative to economic cost. You can see results based on pure DCE willingness to pay, combined DCE and epidemiological outbreak benefits, and endorsement-adjusted effective benefits that weight total benefits by the share of stakeholders who would endorse each configuration.

Copilot helper

This tab prepares a complete text package for Microsoft Copilot based on the scenario you have analysed in STEPS. The prepared text includes a fixed interpretation prompt and a JSON block that summarises your current configuration, including programme tier, cohort size, number of cohorts, mentorship intensity, career incentives, delivery mode, response time, costs, endorsement, willingness to pay, epidemiological outputs, benefit cost ratios and net benefits.

To use this helper, first run your analysis inside STEPS by applying a configuration and reviewing the Results, Costing details, National simulation and Sensitivity / DCE benefits tabs. Once you are satisfied with the scenario, come to this Copilot tab, review the prepared text, then press the copy button and open Microsoft Copilot in a new browser tab. The prepared text is designed to be pasted as a single prompt into Copilot so that it can reconstruct the scenario and generate a structured policy briefing.

The Copilot helper does not store data, does not transmit information automatically and does not send outputs to any third party service. All information remains in your browser until you choose to copy it. Copilot will only receive the scenario details when you paste the prepared text into https://copilot.microsoft.com.

After copying the text below, open https://copilot.microsoft.com in a new tab, choose the most appropriate Copilot mode for policy analysis, and paste the text into the chat box. Copilot will then use the embedded STEPS Tool Copilot Interpretation Prompt and the scenario JSON to produce a policy ready briefing with tables and narrative interpretation of endorsement, willingness to pay, epidemiological benefits, costs, benefit cost ratios and net benefits for national scale up of FETP in India.

About STEPS

The University of Newcastle is developing a business case for strengthening and scaling FETP programmes in India. STEPS is one of the tools in this work. It provides a structured way to test scale-up options and explain cost, benefit and willingness-to-pay implications in policy discussions.

The tool is designed to support the Government of India and partners as they plan investments in epidemiology training. It helps answer questions such as:

  • How do costs, willingness to pay and benefits change when moving from Frontline to Intermediate or Advanced tiers?
  • What happens to endorsement when mentorship intensity is increased?
  • How many cohorts and trainees are needed to reach target scale?

Prepared by Mesfin Genie, PhD, Newcastle Business School, The University of Newcastle, Australia. For questions or clarifications please contact mesfin.genie@newcastle.edu.au.

Advanced settings and assumptions

This panel allows technical users to adjust key multipliers for workshops without editing code. Changes affect calculations immediately in this browser session and are reflected in the Results, National simulation and Sensitivity / DCE benefits tabs.

Use these settings when you want to align STEPS assumptions with updated national estimates or discussions with partner organisations. For example, you can change the indicative value per graduate or per outbreak response, or match the INR to USD display rate to current exchange rates. The technical appendix also sets out how willingness-to-pay is derived from the discrete choice experiment and used as an additional benefit metric.

Used only for currency display. All calculations are in INR.

Country teams can also supply an epi_config.json file with multipliers and indicative values that STEPS will load automatically. This keeps epidemiological and benefit assumptions separate from the main code base.
