What STEPS does

STEPS helps policy makers and partners explore options for scaling Field Epidemiology Training Programmes in India. It links three main inputs:

  • Preferences and willingness to pay estimates from a discrete choice experiment with stakeholders.
  • Cost components and templates that combine submissions from WHO, NIE and NCDC for Frontline, Intermediate and Advanced programmes into a single coherent structure for each tier.
  • Simple epidemiological multipliers that translate training scale up and outbreak response times into health benefits.

Users can set a programme configuration, see endorsement rates, willingness to pay, costs and benefit cost ratios, and compare saved scenarios side by side for business case discussions.

The tool is designed to support transparent conversations with the Government of India and other partners. It keeps all assumptions visible and connects preference evidence directly to programme design, economic summaries and epidemiological implications.

How to use the tool

  1. Go to the Configuration tab and choose programme tier, career incentive, mentorship intensity, response time, delivery mode, number of cohorts, cohort size and cost per trainee per month.
  2. Keep the cost slider within the range used in the experiment (75,000 to 400,000 rupees per trainee per month).
  3. Apply the configuration. The tool uses the mixed logit preference model from the discrete choice experiment to calculate endorsement and willingness to pay values for the chosen design.
  4. Click Apply configuration to update all calculations, then use View results summary to open a scenario summary with headline recommendations.
  5. Save promising scenarios in the Saved scenarios tab and download them as Excel or a policy brief PDF for reporting and World Bank documentation.
  6. For workshops or sensitivity checks, use the National simulation and Sensitivity / DCE benefits tabs to explore how results change with cohorts, endorsement and different benefit definitions, and the Advanced and methods tab to adjust multipliers and open the full technical appendix.

STEPS is designed for laptops, desktops, tablets and mobiles.

Opt out and cost components

Element Definition How STEPS uses it
Opt out alternative
An opt out option where no new FETP training is funded under the scenario being considered.
The discrete choice experiment included an explicit opt out option. STEPS uses this as the benchmark when calculating endorsement. Endorsement is the predicted share choosing the programme configuration rather than opting out.
Cost components
Combined cost components for each tier, covering salary and benefits, travel, training, trainee support and indirect costs including opportunity cost.
For each tier, STEPS uses a combined cost structure that draws on submissions from WHO, NIE and NCDC and aggregates them into a single template. These components are scaled by cohort size and number of cohorts to obtain per cohort and per trainee per month costs for financial and economic perspectives.
Opportunity cost of trainee time
The value of trainee salary time spent in training instead of normal duties, per trainee per month.
When the opportunity cost toggle is on, STEPS adds this to programme cost, yielding total economic cost. When it is off, outputs focus on budgetary cost only. Cost component shares in the costing tab show the contribution of opportunity cost relative to other elements.
Preference model
Mixed logit preference model estimated from the discrete choice experiment.
STEPS uses a mixed logit preference model to predict endorsement of each configuration and to derive willingness to pay measures that feed into economic summaries and sensitivity analysis.

Training B and reference attribute levels form the baseline. The alternative specific constant for Training A captures a layout effect. The opt out constant captures the general tendency to choose neither training option. STEPS uses the opt out constant when predicting overall endorsement of any FETP option versus no FETP.

Endorsement and opt out

These percentages are obtained by plugging the chosen configuration into the mixed logit preference model and comparing the utility of any FETP option with the utility of opting out. They summarise how attractive the configuration is to stakeholders in the preference study.

Endorse FETP option -

Estimated share of stakeholders who would support funding this programme rather than choosing the opt-out option.

Choose opt out -

Estimated share who would prefer not to invest in this option under the assumptions in the study.

Willingness to pay and economic outcomes

Willingness to pay per trainee per month is derived from the preference model by dividing attribute coefficients by the cost coefficient. It indicates how much value stakeholders attach to the bundle of attributes in this configuration relative to a baseline. Total willingness to pay aggregates this value across all trainees for each cohort and at national scale.

Willingness to pay per trainee per month -

Indicative value stakeholders place on this configuration per trainee per month, relative to the baseline design used in the experiment.

Total willingness to pay per cohort -

Willingness to pay per trainee per month multiplied by trainees per cohort and programme duration, treating stakeholder preferences as an additional benefit measure.

Programme cost per cohort -

Direct programme cost per cohort before adding any opportunity cost of trainee time.

Total economic cost per cohort -

Programme cost plus opportunity cost where applicable. This is the main cost figure used in benefit cost ratios.

Net benefit per cohort (epidemiological) -

Indicative epidemiological benefit per cohort minus total economic cost per cohort, combining graduate and outbreak response benefits. Willingness to pay is reported separately as a preference based benefit metric.

Benefit cost ratio (per cohort) -

Total indicative epidemiological benefit per cohort divided by total economic cost per cohort. Values above one suggest that epidemiological benefits exceed costs on the assumptions used.

Epidemiological implications

Graduates and outbreak responses are obtained by combining endorsement with simple multipliers on cohort size and number of cohorts. Indicative benefit per cohort converts additional graduates and outbreak responses into monetary terms using values that can be adjusted in the Advanced and methods tab. The expected response time attribute from the discrete choice experiment is used to interpret how quickly these benefits accrue and to structure discussions about the value of faster detection.

FETP graduates (all cohorts) -

Estimated number of graduates produced across all planned cohorts, adjusting for endorsement and completion rates.

Outbreak responses per year -

Approximate number of outbreak responses that could be supported per year once all graduates are in post under this configuration.

Indicative epidemiological benefit per cohort -

Monetary value of graduates and outbreak responses per cohort using the values specified in the Advanced and methods tab or epidemiological configuration file.

Cost structure and sources

Cost components in STEPS are based on harmonised submissions from WHO, NIE and NCDC and are combined into a single cost template for each tier. The template for each tier covers direct cost categories such as salary and benefits, training, equipment, travel and trainee support, plus indirect cost categories and explicit opportunity cost.

Instead of choosing separate templates for individual institutions, the tool uses a combined template for each tier that reflects total programme costs across all institutions and the corresponding shares of each component. These combined components are then scaled by cohort size, number of cohorts and programme duration to derive per cohort and per trainee per month costs.

When the opportunity cost switch in the Configuration tab is on, components marked as opportunity cost are included to form total economic cost. When the switch is off, results focus on financial costs only. Interpret scenarios with Microsoft Copilot

This tab prepares a combined interpretation prompt and scenario JSON so that Microsoft Copilot can produce a structured policy briefing. The STEPS tool never sends data automatically. You review the full text, copy it when you are ready and paste it into Copilot in a separate browser tab.

How to use Copilot with STEPS

First, use the other STEPS tabs to define a scenario that you want to interpret. Apply the configuration, review endorsement, willingness to pay, costs and epidemiological benefits, and check the national simulation and sensitivity views. Once you have a scenario that is relevant for policy discussions, move to this Copilot tab to prepare a narrative briefing.

When you press the Copilot button, STEPS regenerates the interpretation text using the latest scenario and model outputs. The prompt combines a short description of the STEPS tool, a set of instructions for Copilot and the full JSON export for the current scenario. The aim is to guide Copilot to prepare a three to five page policy brief that is ready for meetings with ministries, World Bank staff and other partners.

The policy brief that Copilot produces should read as a narrative report rather than as a list of bullet points. The interpretation request asks for clearly labelled sections in paragraph form, such as background, scenario description, endorsement and preference patterns, costs, epidemiological benefits, benefit cost ratios, net present values, distributional considerations and implementation notes. Copilot is also asked to include tables summarising key indicators while keeping the main text concise and accessible.

After copying the text from the dark prompt panel, open Microsoft Copilot in a new browser tab, paste the entire content and run it. You can then edit the draft policy brief inside Copilot or in your preferred word processing software, keeping a clear record of the assumptions and JSON values generated by STEPS.

The JSON export is limited to variables that are necessary for interpretation, such as configuration choices, endorsement, cost components, epidemiological multipliers and benefit indicators. The technical appendix lists these fields and explains how they are derived from the discrete choice experiment, cost templates and epidemiological assumptions.

About STEPS

The University of Newcastle is developing a business case for strengthening and scaling FETP programmes in India. STEPS is one of the tools in this work. It provides a structured way to test scale up options and explain cost, benefit and willingness to pay implications in policy discussions.

The tool is designed to support the Government of India and partners as they plan investments in epidemiology training. It helps answer questions such as:

  • How do costs, willingness to pay and benefits change when moving from Frontline to Intermediate or Advanced tiers?
  • What happens to endorsement when mentorship intensity is increased?
  • How many cohorts and trainees are needed to reach target scale?

Prepared by Mesfin Genie, PhD, Newcastle Business School, The University of Newcastle, Australia. For questions or clarifications please contact mesfin.genie@newcastle.edu.au.

Methods overview and technical appendix

STEPS combines three elements into a single decision aid for FETP scale up in India:

  • A discrete choice experiment with stakeholders that provides endorsement rates and willingness to pay for alternative programme designs.
  • Combined cost components for each tier that reflect total programme costs across institutions, disaggregated into direct, indirect and opportunity cost categories.
  • Simple epidemiological multipliers that connect graduates and response times to outbreak response capacity and monetised benefits.

The main calculations follow standard economic evaluation practice. Programme and economic costs are derived from cost components and cohort structure. Willingness to pay is derived from the discrete choice experiment by dividing attribute coefficients by the cost coefficient. Epidemiological benefits are obtained by combining graduates, outbreak response multipliers and indicative values per graduate and per outbreak. Benefit cost ratios and net present values are then calculated by comparing benefit streams with economic costs over the chosen planning horizon.

For full details, including model specification, estimation outputs, formulae and worked examples, please refer to the technical appendix. Open the technical appendix in a new tab.
